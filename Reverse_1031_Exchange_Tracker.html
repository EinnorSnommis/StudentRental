The Big Picture
A quick summary of what we're doing and why.
What is a Reverse 1031 Exchange? We want to buy a new investment property before selling our current condo. Normally you sell first, then buy. A Reverse Exchange flips that order — buy first, sell second — while still deferring the capital gains taxes on the condo sale. The IRS doesn't allow us to own both properties simultaneously during the exchange, so a third-party company (the EAT) temporarily holds title to one property while the deal is completed.
What is an Improvement Exchange? Since the new property ($299,900) costs less than our condo (~$350,000), the ~$50,000 difference would normally be taxable ("boot"). An Improvement Exchange lets us use the leftover exchange funds for renovations. If purchase + completed renovations ≥ condo sale price, we eliminate the boot and defer all taxes.
Why the Rush? We want the property renovated and rent-ready for college student athletes by August 2026. This requires acting quickly — engaging professionals, securing financing, and starting renovations as soon as possible.

How It Works (6 Steps)

  1. Engage a Qualified Intermediary (QI) — they set up the exchange structure and create the EAT. Must happen before closing.
  2. EAT purchases the new property — we fund it via HELOC, bridge loan, or mortgage. We don't technically own it yet.
  3. Renovations happen while EAT holds title — exchange funds pay contractors. Only completed, installed work counts.
  4. Within 45 days — formally identify the condo as the property to sell.
  5. Sell the condo within 180 days — proceeds flow through the QI.
  6. Exchange closes — EAT transfers title to us. We own the property.
Good News: We Can Rent During the Exchange While the EAT holds title, they lease the property to us. We can sublease to student athletes and start collecting rent even before the exchange is fully completed.

1967 House Concerns
Critical items to inspect and address for a 59-year-old home. Check off items as they're investigated.
  • Lead-Based Paint Inspection Banned in 1978. A 1967 home almost certainly has it. Must use EPA lead-safe renovation practices (RRP rule). Required before any renovation work. Especially important since we're renting to young adults. CRITICAL — SCHEDULE FIRST
  • Asbestos Testing Common in 1960s construction: floor tiles (9×9"), pipe insulation, textured ceilings, adhesives, roofing. Safe when undisturbed but dangerous during renovation. Professional abatement cost counts toward exchange improvements. CRITICAL — SCHEDULE FIRST
  • Electrical System Inspection May have undersized panel (60–100 amp vs. modern 200 amp), insufficient outlets, or aluminum wiring. Six bedrooms of student athletes need adequate capacity. Panel upgrade: $2,000–$5,000+. IMPORTANT
  • Plumbing Inspection Original 1967 plumbing may include galvanized steel pipes that corrode internally. Check water flow, pipe material, and condition before opening walls for bathroom renovations. Public water/sewer confirmed. IMPORTANT
  • Crawl Space Inspection Crawl space foundation in NC's humid climate = moisture, mold, and pest risk. Check for standing water, moisture barriers, insulation, mold, and pest damage. Encapsulation work counts as exchange improvement. IMPORTANT
  • Roof Age & Condition Not listed. Roofs last 20–30 years. Ask seller/agent for age and last replacement. A new roof ($8,000–$15,000+) is a major exchange-qualifying improvement. IMPORTANT
  • HVAC System Age & Condition Gas heating and cooling listed. HVAC units last 15–20 years. Get age and maintenance history. Replacement ($5,000–$12,000) counts as exchange improvement and improves rental appeal. IMPORTANT
  • Insulation Assessment 1960s insulation often inadequate by modern standards. Check attic and crawl space. Upgrading improves energy efficiency and tenant comfort. CHECK DURING INSPECTION
  • Foundation Check 59 years of settling. Look for cracks, uneven floors, sticking doors. Foundation issues can be very costly. Include in general home inspection. CHECK DURING INSPECTION
  • Water Heater Age Typical lifespan 10–15 years. If old, replacement ($1,000–$3,000) counts as improvement and is essential for a rental with 4–6 tenants. CHECK DURING INSPECTION
